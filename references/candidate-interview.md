# Candidate Interview Reference

## Intake principle

Treat the user's free-form description, preferences, worries, values, corrections, portfolio links, and voice transcript as primary material. A resume is optional and never the sole source of truth.

The local agent must actively discover the user's needs and experience. Do not reduce the user to resume keywords.

## Needs discovery

Extract and keep visible:

- target role direction and why the user wants it;
- roles, industries, or work styles the user dislikes;
- preferred city, remote/hybrid/on-site pattern, commute tolerance;
- compensation expectation and minimum acceptable level;
- company preferences: big company, startup, state-owned, foreign company, research lab, product company, outsourcing, consulting, or other;
- risk tolerance: instability, overtime, travel, ambiguous role, unclear business, probation risk;
- learning appetite and time budget for 30/90/180-day improvement;
- timing: urgent job search, exploratory search, internal transfer, career pivot, or long-term preparation;
- deal-breakers that should block a company or role even when skill fit is high.

## Experience discovery

For each meaningful experience, identify:

- the problem being solved;
- the user's responsibility boundary;
- decisions the user personally made;
- tools, methods, models, systems, or workflows used;
- constraints and trade-offs;
- validation method and failure handling;
- outcome, using public metrics when available and safe abstraction when private;
- evidence type: public artifact, internal/private proof, verbal-only explanation, in-progress work, planned work, or disallowed material;
- whether the fact can be used in a resume, interview only, abstract-only, or not at all.

## Living profile

Maintain these sections across turns:

- confirmed experience and scope;
- technical depth and production responsibility;
- public, private, and missing evidence;
- preferences, dislikes, values, and desired environment;
- location, compensation, education, timing, and other constraints;
- target directions and reasons;
- company first-gate preferences and deal-breakers;
- uncertainties and contradictions;
- planned learning or projects that must not be presented as completed.

Attach a source turn or file to material facts. When the user corrects a fact, retain the correction history and use the latest confirmed version.

## Follow-up selection

Ask only questions with high decision value. Prioritize:

1. facts that trigger a hard filter;
2. preferences that change company ranking;
3. responsibility boundaries and independent contribution;
4. production scale, reliability, evaluation, and measurable or safely generalized outcomes;
5. confidentiality boundaries and safe evidence;
6. contradictions between files and later statements.

Ask no more than three questions in one round. Explain briefly what decision each answer affects. Do not block the entire analysis when reasonable partial conclusions are possible.

## Confidential work

Ask for architecture, constraints, trade-offs, validation method, failure handling, and the user's decisions at an abstract level. Do not request source code, proprietary datasets, client identities, unreleased algorithms, or sensitive production metrics.

Turn private depth into safe evidence such as:

- problem and operational constraint;
- responsibility boundary;
- alternatives considered;
- validation and monitoring method;
- generalized outcome;
- what can be demonstrated with a synthetic or open-source reproduction.
