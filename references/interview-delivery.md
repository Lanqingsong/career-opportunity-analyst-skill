# Interview and Evidence Preparation

## Build the question tree

Cover four layers:

1. HR: motivation, direction choice, compensation, location, education, tenure, and apparent inconsistencies.
2. Hiring manager: responsibility boundary, independent decisions, delivery ownership, collaboration, failure handling, and business effect.
3. Technical interviewer: architecture, alternatives, evaluation design, performance, reliability, observability, debugging, deployment, and edge cases.
4. Career planner: structural limits, reachable gaps, evidence plan, and whether the chosen direction remains worth the investment.

Prioritize questions that can invalidate a resume claim or change the direction decision.

## Evidence levels

Label support as:

- `public_artifact`: repository, article, video, paper, patent, demo, benchmark, or published document;
- `safe_private_summary`: truthful abstract description that respects confidentiality;
- `verbal_only`: candidate explanation without an inspectable artifact;
- `planned_reproduction`: future public evidence, not current proof;
- `missing`: no support supplied.

Do not treat a patent author position, paper author position, technology keyword, or project name alone as proof of depth. Ask what the candidate personally designed, implemented, validated, deployed, debugged, and improved.

## Safe answer frame

For confidential work, prepare answers using:

1. public problem class;
2. candidate responsibility boundary;
3. non-sensitive constraints;
4. alternatives and trade-offs at an abstract level;
5. validation method;
6. generalized outcome;
7. explicit disclosure boundary.

Also provide a short refusal sentence for requests that cross the boundary, followed by a safe substitute such as a synthetic reproduction, public paper, architecture sketch, or generic failure case.

## Professional checks

Generate checks from the target role's real requirements. For each check record:

- capability being tested;
- expected depth for the role;
- question;
- strong-answer anchors;
- weak or evasive signals;
- candidate evidence that can support the answer;
- preparation task when evidence is weak.

Do not present quiz performance as proof of production experience. Use it to identify explanation and knowledge gaps.
